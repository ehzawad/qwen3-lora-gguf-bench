What a real chatbot needs (from the 2025–2026 literature — not our measurement)

Prefix caching is the top lever for multi-turn

Real chats are multi-turn: the system prompt + history is a huge shared prefix, each new turn a tiny suffix. With prefix caching (automatic in vLLM/SGLang) only the uncached suffix is prefilled. Our E3 deliberately disabled it (--no-cache-prompt) — measuring the worst case (4.3∶1 prefill∶decode). One multi-turn KV-reuse system cuts P99 TTFT up to −69% vs vLLM/SGLang KV-cache baselines (SwiftCache). Enable it before tuning anything else.

TTFT & TPOT beat aggregate throughput for UX

TTFT = time to first token; TPOT = inter-token latency (streaming smoothness). At our throughput peak (C=64) aggregate tok/s is maximal but each user waits ~20 s. The production metric is goodput — requests meeting a latency SLO — not raw throughput. Pick your operating point from an SLO.

Use vLLM / SGLang when…

  • many concurrent users on a GPU, latency SLAs
  • you want PagedAttention + native continuous batching + prefix caching + speculative decoding
  • native multi-LoRA (many adapters, one base)

Use llama.cpp when…

  • single-user desktop / edge / offline
  • a quick prototype or model-swapping via GGUF
  • CPU or mixed hardware, trivial deployment
The caveat on the scary numbersA Red Hat test cites ~44× tok/s and stable sub-second TTFT at 64 users for vLLM — but that used Llama-3.1-8B full-precision on one H200 (very different from our 4B/Q6_K/A5000), and the source calls it config-specific. Our -cb, --parallel==C llama.cpp had p99 TTFT ~7.5 s at C=128 (not 180 s), yet still hit a host-bound wall. vLLM's scheduler is designed to improve high-concurrency serving; whether it would push past this rig's wall is unmeasured — no engine winner is stated.

Speed vs accuracy, and the latest levers

Quantization: Q6_K near-lossless; at 4-bit, GGUF-Q4/AWQ/GPTQ are close and model/task/calibration-dependent (AWQ often matches/beats GPTQ per its paper, ~1.45× kernel speedup). FP8: the A5000 lacks native FP8 W8A8 acceleration (needs Ada/Hopper), though Ampere runs FP8 weight-only via Marlin. The 2025–26 levers — speculative decoding, chunked prefill, prefix caching, disaggregated prefill/decode (vLLM still labels the last experimental) — typically move TTFT and effective concurrency more than a faster quant.

Bottom lineOur numbers are a floor: one engine, prefix cache off, no speculative decoding. On the right engine with prefix caching on, a multi-turn chatbot's TTFT under load is substantially better than our E3 worst case — by how much on this rig is unmeasured.
